Axiom Structure

PMN operates on three tiers. Tier 1 axioms are design choices defended by argument. Tier 2 are structural commitments revisable only by architectural failure. Tier 3 are empirical hypotheses explicitly revisable.

● Tier 1 — Foundational
● Tier 2 — Structural
● Tier 3 — Empirical
Tier 1 — Foundational Axioms (adopted as starting points)
Reality exists independently of perception or conceptual framework. This is the ground-clearing move: any framework that begins with values or norms before establishing what is materially the case has built on sand. Ref: 2.1
The presence of suffering is bad and its reduction is good. This is a design choice defended by argument — not uniquely determined by it — but it provides the non-arbitrary evaluative anchor the framework requires. Without this commitment, the framework produces structural analysis without evaluative direction. Ref: 3.0, 3.3
The expansion of capacity for development beyond survival is not merely instrumentally valuable — it is the evaluative ceiling that pairs with the suffering floor. A framework that addresses only the floor produces a politics of permanent emergency; one that addresses only the ceiling ignores who is being destroyed by its absence. Ref: 4.5b
Tier 2 — Structural Commitments (revisable only by architectural failure)
Life as ontological foundation, suffering as evaluative criterion, becoming as evaluative ceiling. These three levels are structurally interdependent — you cannot remove one without collapsing the framework's evaluative architecture. The floor and ceiling are not competing values; they are the same commitment at different scales. Ref: 3.0
Organisms avoid pain and seek resources as a prior fact, before any normative framework is imposed. This is not a claim that organisms are selfish in all their behavior — it is the minimal factual anchor from which the is-ought bridge begins. The claim is about structural tendency, not universal behavior. Ref: 3.2
Ecological Viability → Biological Floor → Economic Arrangements → Institutional Architecture → Meaning Infrastructure. Each layer is constrained by the layer below it. Institutional design that violates biological constraints fails. Economic arrangements that violate ecological constraints collapse. The hierarchy is not a value ranking — it is a dependency structure. Ref: 2.2
Structural forces establish probability distributions over outcomes, not specific outcomes. Agents are nodes of probability in material networks, not free-floating will-bearers. This prevents moral frameworks from punishing individuals for outcomes produced by structural conditions, and redirects analysis toward the conditions themselves. Ref: 1.3
Suffering at scale produces observable systemic instability. This is the bridge between descriptive and normative: arrangements that produce mass suffering are not merely evaluated as bad — they generate measurable material consequences that can be tracked independently of moral intuition. The evaluative claim is anchored in causal claims about stability. Ref: 3.3
Source level (material processes as they are) / interface level (how agents experience and represent those processes). The distinction prevents category errors: values, meaning, and experience are real at the interface level while being causally produced at the source level. Reducing one to the other is an error in both directions. Ref: 2.3
Tier 3 — Empirical Hypotheses (explicitly revisable)
The permanent conditions identified in Part III are genuinely permanent rather than historically contingent — working hypothesis, falsifiable by demonstration of institutional designs that eliminate them. If someone shows that scarcity, information asymmetry, or the custodian problem can be durably resolved by institutional design, this assumption requires revision. Ref: Part III
Speculative projection, stated as such. The framework does not require this to be true for its analytical claims to hold — it is a claim about developmental trajectory, not a precondition for the evaluative criterion or structural analysis. Ref: Part IX
Assumed on convergence grounds — the biological foundation of suffering is universal in a way that culturally specific values are not. The falsification condition in 14.4b specifies the evidence that would revise this: demonstration that organisms with equivalent neurological architecture systematically fail to avoid suffering, or that suffering does not produce the systemic instability the bridge claim requires. Ref: 3.2, 14.4b
The formulas in Part XV are structural hypotheses, not confirmed causal laws. They are tools for identifying which variables are most analytically significant in a given situation, not mechanisms for generating precise predictions. Their value is heuristic — they force explicitness about which structural factors are being invoked and why. Ref: Part XV
